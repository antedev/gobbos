# Researcher Report: Crafting Systems in TTRPGs
**Topic:** Crafting systems that are easy, fun, highly customizable, emphasize scrap hunting over monetary purchases, and involve "fun matching" to create new gear.

## Executive Summary
Historically, crafting in TTRPGs falls into two traps: it is either too abstracted (just roll a die and subtract gold) or overly complex (requiring spreadsheets of distinct reagents like "3x Iron, 1x Wolf Pelt"). 

For a game emphasizing chaotic, "scrap-built" ingenuity—perfect for goblins—the best systems abstract the *materials* into generic "Scrap" or "Parts," but emphasize the *components' traits* to allow for "fun matching" and combinatorial creativity.

Below is an analysis of systems that successfully balance simplicity with deep, rewarding customization.

---

## 1. Break!! RPG (The "Fun Matching" Gold Standard)
**The Mechanic:** `Break!!` has a brilliantly simple crafting/upgrading system where you combine two distinct objects to create a new one. The newly forged item inherits both the *advantages* and the *disadvantages* (or quirks) of the components used.
*   **Why it works:** It requires zero complex math or material tracking. 
*   **Scrap Hunting:** Players hunt for items with specific, interesting tags rather than raw crafting materials.
*   **Goblin Application:** Taping a "Heavy Hammer" to a "Firecracker" yields a "Heavy Firecracker Hammer" that does massive damage but might explode in your hands (inheriting the volatile nature of the firecracker). It directly gamifies the "fun matching."

## 2. Mutant: Year Zero (The "Scrap Survival" Standard)
**The Mechanic:** Players explore the post-apocalyptic Zone and scavenge exactly what it sounds like: *Scrap*. The "Gearhead" class uses this generic Scrap to jury-rig temporary, highly effective weapons and vehicles. 
*   **Why it works:** It forces players to constantly hunt for junk. Equipment degrades over time and breaks, meaning players are trapped in a constant loop of finding scrap and building new, temporary solutions.
*   **Scrap Hunting:** Scrap is the primary currency and lifeline of the game. You don't buy things; you build them from garbage.
*   **Goblin Application:** Goblins building crude, temporary death-machines out of garbage fits perfectly. A system where weapons naturally degrade on natural 1s, requiring more scrap to repair or invent a new replacement, creates a continuous cycle of scavenging.

## 3. Index Card RPG / ICRPG (The "MacGyver" Approach)
**The Mechanic:** ICRPG focuses heavily on "Loot" rather than character stats. The crafting system usually boils down to the GM and player agreeing on combining two pieces of Loot, requiring a certain amount of "Effort" (a mechanical track) to complete the construction.
*   **Why it works:** It is entirely narrative but mechanically grounded by the "Effort" system (e.g., this crazy invention takes "2 Hearts" of crafting effort to finish, meaning the player has to spend actions during downtime or even mid-combat to assemble it).
*   **Goblin Application:** It gives a mechanical pacing to crafting. Building a "Scrap-apult" mid-combat takes 3 successful crafting actions, giving the rest of the mob time to defend the crafter.

## 4. Numenera / Cypher System (The "Weird Tech" Approach)
**The Mechanic:** The world is littered with "Cyphers" (one-time use, highly specific pieces of magical/technological junk). The *Numenera Destiny* supplement introduced a crafting system based on "Iotum"—abstracted salvage like "Io" or "Quantium" used to build installations.
*   **Why it works:** Cyphers force players to use weird combinations of items because they can only carry a few at a time. If they tape an "Anti-gravity emitter" to a "Plasma cell," weird things happen.
*   **Goblin Application:** Treating "Scrap" not just as metal, but as weird, volatile components (e.g., "Shiny Rock," "Bouncy Fungus," "Angry Bug") allows players to mix and match to see what chaotic effect occurs.

---

## Synthesis & Recommendations for "Gobbos"

Based on the research, a "not overly complex, fun matching, scrap-hunting" system should embrace the following framework:

### A. Abstract the Currency, Specify the Tags
Do not track "wood" vs "iron." Instead, players scavenge **"Scrap"** (the generic fuel for crafting) and **"Oddities/Bits"** (unique items that provide specific Tags).
*   *Example:* A player finds 3 Scrap and 1 "Bouncy" Oddity. 

### B. The "Duct Tape" Rule (From Break!!)
Crafting should literally be combining two items/tags to generate a new item with both properties.
*   **Recipe:** Base Item + Oddity + [X] Scrap = New Toy.
*   *Example:* Base "Choppa" (Damage 1) + "Bouncy" Oddity + 2 Scrap = "Bouncy Choppa" (Can be thrown and bounces back, but might hit the user on a fail).

### C. Volatility and Degradation (From Mutant: Year Zero)
Goblin tech shouldn't last forever. Powerful crafted combinations should have a risk of breaking down, forcing the goblins to continually hunt for more scrap rather than hoarding a perfect weapon forever. 

### Output Summary
The most successful systems for this specific niche avoid deep simulation. They provide players with a sandbox of descriptive *tags* and a generic currency (*scrap/effort*), trusting the players and GM to organically create chaotic, unique, and highly breakable equipment.
