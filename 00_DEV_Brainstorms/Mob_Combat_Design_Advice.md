# Mob Combat - OSR Design Advice

When designing rules for "mob combat" with an Old-School Renaissance (OSR) or B/X D&D philosophy, the primary goal is speed, player agency, and logical adjudication over complex simulation. 

Here is advice based on successful OSR TTRPG design patterns:

## 1. Abstraction and Scaling
OSR combat design avoids tracking individual hit points for a swarm.
*   **The Single Unit:** Treat 10 or 20 weaker creatures as a single, more powerful entity stat-wise. A mob of 10 goblins might just be a single "Goblin Mob" monster with 5 Hit Dice.
*   **Thresholds:** As the mob takes damage, its numbers dwindle. You don't need to track exactly which goblin dies, just describe them falling as the HP goes down.

## 2. Simplified Attack Rolls
To speed up play, eliminate rolling 10 individual d20s.
*   **Guaranteed Hits:** Based on the mob's size and the target's Armor Class, determine a set number of automatic hits. (e.g., if 10 goblins attack AC 15, assume 3 hit automatically without rolling).
*   **Aggregate Damage Calculation:** Calculate the average damage output for a unit based on the individual creature's damage and apply it directly.
*   **One-Roll Resolution:** Have the entire mob roll a single d20. On a hit, they deal damage scaled to their current size/HP.

## 3. Morale is Critical
*   **Morale Checks:** This is vital in OSR mob combat. When an enemy unit suffers significant losses (e.g., first casualty, losing half their numbers), they must roll a Morale check (usually 2d6 under their Morale score). If they fail, the mob breaks and flees. You rarely fight a mob to the last man.

## 4. Player Impact and Rulings
*   **Rulings, Not Rules:** Allow players to use the environment, clever traps, or fire to break the mob's formation instead of just swinging swords.
*   **Focus on the Goal:** Mob combat is often an obstacle to a goal, not a fight to the death. Give players a way to route the mob by taking out its leader or using loud noises, emphasizing player skill over character statistics.
